Last October, we took part in the MIT Media Lab's [workshop on designing benchmarks for human flourishing with AI](/blog/aha-benchmarks-workshop). The resulting report, *[Towards Open Benchmarks for Human Flourishing with AI](https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/report-benchmarks-for-human-flourishing-with-ai/overview/)*, is now officially published, with contributions from workshop participants — including Vid and Isobel.

The report synthesises what 80 experts from over 40 institutions produced across the workshop's three dimensions of flourishing — reasoning, comprehension and agency; curiosity and learning; and healthy emotional and social lives. It includes:

- A taxonomy of **26 risk categories and 24 opportunity categories** for AI's impact on human flourishing
- **Concrete benchmark scenarios** with operationalised appropriate and inappropriate model behaviours
- A **roadmap** for collaborative platform development, real-world data collection, and model optimisation

The initiative hasn't stopped at the report. Taking the workshop's call for open, collaborative development to heart, a team from MIT, USC, UC Berkeley, and the Psychology of Technology Institute has been building [**ImpactBench**](https://impactbench.media.mit.edu/), an open benchmark of AI's impact on humans. It currently spans 18 expert-submitted benchmarks covering psychological, physical, and societal effects of AI — from emotional dependence and cognitive autonomy to health and child safety — evaluated through multi-turn simulation with realistic, demographically varied personas. Results are presented as a "nutritional label" designed to be readable by parents, teachers, policymakers, and users, not just researchers. Benchmarks are contributed through an [open submission process](https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/the-open-benchmark-for-the-human-impact-of-ai/overview/), with a public launch to follow.

We're excited to see agency featured so prominently in how the AI community is starting to measure what matters — and we'll keep contributing a child-centred perspective to it.
