On Tuesday 17 February, we ran a workshop with 16 teenagers at the [Science Oxford](https://scienceoxford.com/) centre, as part of their three-day Insight event — which the students had applied to join themselves. The young people came from schools across east Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire, predominantly state schools and mostly from underserved communities.

The workshop is part of our [Youth PRISM study](/blog/study-genai), which investigates two questions: what are teenagers' current experiences with large language models, and what makes those experiences positive or negative? And what values drive teenagers' decision-making about — and during — the use of generative AI?

After a short introduction to the Oxford Child-Centred AI group, the students worked through three activities in small groups:

1. **A values survey.** Which AI tools do they use, and how often? Then the harder part: ranking eight values by importance to them — *factual*, *understandable*, *trustworthy*, *safe*, *fair*, *diverse*, *helpful*, and *fun*.

2. **Scenario cards.** Posters around the room presented scenarios where someone might use AI, and students placed stickers to show whether they would or wouldn't use it in each one. We then discussed the extremes together — the scenarios everyone agreed on, and the ones that split the room evenly — digging into the values behind each choice, and into when using AI feels good or leaves a bad experience.

3. **Hands-on with [Dynabench](https://dynabench.org/).** Students brought their own task to an LLM, wrote prompts, and compared responses from different models side by side. We probed their reasoning as they went: how do you choose between models? What surprises you? At the end, they reflected on how they would *want* to feel when using AI, and on how their peers influence their choices — before returning to the values survey to see whether the hands-on experience had changed their rankings.

We wrapped up with a plenary and an audience Q&A. All 16 students joined the activities; we recorded research data only from the 13 who chose to take part in the study.

Our thanks to Science Oxford for hosting us, and to the students for their energy and honesty. The findings will feed into the wider Youth PRISM study — look out for more updates here.
