Yesterday, Vid attended the Festival of Computing in Bromsgrove — a conference bringing together around 200 computing teachers from across England, along with organisations like the Raspberry Pi Foundation, Digit&lt;all&gt;, and Oak National Academy, and representatives from the OCR, AQA, and Pearson exam boards. As you might expect, AI was a big topic throughout. Here are some takeaways.

![The main hall at the Festival of Computing during a talk](/content/blog/images/festival-of-computing.jpg "The main hall at the Festival of Computing")

## Agency, everywhere

Even when the word wasn't used, children's agency ran through the AI discussions:

- One framing put agency in terms of positive and negative liberties: negative liberty removes barriers (system limitations, adult restrictions, infrastructure), while positive liberty empowers people to achieve what they want (goal setting, AI literacy, critical thinking, computational thinking).
- "The LLM is responsive, not responsible" — *you* are responsible for your use of AI outputs and their impacts.
- Anthropomorphising chips away at user agency by ascribing undue agency to the system. One suggestion: teach pupils not to prompt with "I want *you* to…" but with "I want…" or "I need help with…". It's a hard habit to shift, because anthropomorphised systems are easier to think about.
- A neat ladder of prompts for progressively less support: "do this for me" → "give me ideas to choose from" → "ask me questions, so I can come up with ideas".
- Stanford's three pillars of AI use — user, developer, critic — with the Raspberry Pi Foundation arguing that AI literacy underlies all three. Their [Experience AI](https://experience-ai.org/) materials map learning objectives to UNESCO's AI competency framework for students, and their [Prompt a large language model](https://projects.raspberrypi.org/en/projects/prompt-a-large-language-model) project gives young people a hands-on start.
- Encountering AI-generated content is much more common than deliberately using AI tools — most often on social media. Perhaps research should look at AI *exposure*, not just direct use. Some discussions focused on distinguishing AI from non-AI media; our view is that how information was created matters less than whether it is true, honest, and representative.

## From the staffroom

![A conference slide asking why kids attend Code Club, with answers ranging from "my parents make me do a club every day" to "I want to learn to code"](/content/blog/images/festival-of-computing-codeclub.jpg "Why do kids attend Code Club?")

Conversations with teachers grounded all of this in classroom reality. Whatever teachers personally think about AI, they are constrained by exam board policies: if AI use isn't allowed in assessment, they have to teach children to work without it. Goal setting around AI becomes more relevant from Year 9, when pupils start making choices about their GCSE outcomes — at Key Stage 3, AI is mostly something to play with. And one teacher offered a provocation we're still chewing on: "kids have too much agency at my school".

## Sector news

- All three England exam boards — OCR, AQA, and Pearson — are waiting for the DfE curriculum review, with the report due in September.
- OCR expect to eventually follow the SQA in allowing referenced AI use in exams.
- [Oak National Academy](https://www.thenational.academy/) are creating an updated computing curriculum, expected in August. It is topic-based rather than competency-based, so there isn't much in it about responsible AI use.
- The [National Centre for Computing Education](https://teachcomputing.org/) offers free on-demand CPD for teachers on teaching generative AI.

Meeting so many of the teachers who put AI in front of young people every day was invaluable — the questions they're wrestling with are exactly the ones CHAILD hopes to help answer.
